This research is part of a larger collaborative effort called PolyIC, an open-access online platform built to help the broader polymer community develop and apply polymer interaction chromatography (PIC). The long-term ambition is to support this with an AI system capable of answering practical questions with high accuracy, such as whether a given set of conditions will reach the critical point, or what new solvent ratio would be needed after swapping one solvent for another.

PIC is an umbrella term covering several polymer chromatography methods, including liquid chromatography at the critical condition (LCCC). Its value is that it can separate and characterize polymers by features that size and weight-based methods can't resolve, such as chemical composition, end-group makeup, and block lengths, and it's also powerful for detecting synthesis impurities. The catch is that PIC is hard to use: finding the right mobile phase, tuning solvents and temperature near the critical point, and interpreting the results all take significant trial and error, not to mention cost. PolyIC exists to lower those barriers by building on shared knowledge from literature and labs, learning interactions from experimental data, and developing practical, application-driven workflows.

My role is on the machine learning and data analysis side. Before any system can learn from experimental data or answer those advanced questions, the underlying data has to be understood. That means I go through the data set and find important trends, gaps, or inconsistencies and take care of them before feeding that data into a machine learning algorithm to identify relationships. That groundwork is the stage I'm focused on now, and it's what most of the posts on this blog document focus on too.
